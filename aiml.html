Pixel Phantoms at Touché: Ideology and Power Identification in Parliamentary Debates using Linear SVC

This study titled "Pixel Phantoms at Touché: Ideology and Power Identification in Parliamentary Debates using Linear SVC" delves into the nuanced analysis of parliamentary discourse by focusing on two central tasks: identifying the political ideology of speakers and categorizing their party affiliation as either governing or opposition. The research leverages a range of machine learning models, including Linear SVC, Logistic Regression, and DistilBERT, to address these classification challenges. By employing a multilingual corpus sourced from ParlaMint, the study assesses the performance of these models using F1 scores.

The findings reveal that Linear SVC outperforms the other models, demonstrating its effectiveness in managing and interpreting complex political texts. This research underscores the value of integrating both traditional and advanced machine learning methods to enhance the analysis of political discourse.

A Transformer Based Approach for Text-to-Picto Generation

Github link? Not yet done

This research work titled "A Transformer Based Approach for Text-to-Picto Generation" focuses on developing a system that translates French text into corresponding pictogram representations. Utilizing the Google-T5 transformer model, the study fine-tunes the model on a custom French text dataset to accurately generate pictogram terms. The model underwent extensive fine-tuning across multiple epochs to enhance its translation capabilities.

Performance metrics, including PictoER score, BLEU score, and METEOR score, were employed to evaluate the system, with the model achieving notable results: a PictoER score of 13.9, a BLEU score of 74.3, and a METEOR score of 87.0. This approach highlights the effectiveness of transformer-based models in bridging text and visual representation.

Disaster prediction Model

We developed a model designed to address the challenge of identifying disaster-related tweets on Twitter. Given the critical role Twitter plays in real-time communication during emergencies, with many individuals using it to report and discuss observed disasters, it has become increasingly important for agencies like disaster relief organizations and news agencies to monitor these tweets effectively.

Our model focuses on distinguishing tweets that genuinely announce or discuss disasters from those that do not. It leverages advanced machine learning techniques to analyze tweet content and context, applying various algorithms to classify tweets accurately. By training the model on a diverse dataset of labeled tweets, the system learns to recognize patterns and keywords indicative of disaster-related information.

The model achieved an accuracy of 0.783, demonstrating its effectiveness in identifying relevant tweets while minimizing false positives and false negatives. This approach ensures that the model can reliably filter out tweets crucial for emergency response efforts, enhancing the efficiency and responsiveness of disaster monitoring systems.
